Project Overview

The Problem & The Solution

Sleep Aura is a personalized wellness dashboard designed to help university students understand their sleep patterns through data — not just log them. It uses a weighted scoring algorithm, interactive visualizations, and optional AI-driven coaching to bridge the gap between tracking and actual behavioral change.

The Problem

Over 60% of university students identify poor sleep as their primary barrier to academic success. Existing sleep apps are built for the general public — they don't account for the unique university lifestyle: late nights, early classes, and exam-related stress spikes.

Our Solution

A centralized dashboard for students to log sleep, mood, caffeine, and energy levels. Sleep Aura uses data visualization and AI-driven insights to explain why sleep quality fluctuates — and provides actionable recommendations, not just numbers.

Process Documentation

How It Was Built

From initial research to a working open-source tool — here's how Sleep Aura evolved across four phases.

Phase 1 · Validation
Identifying the Real Gap

Researched existing wellness apps and surveyed the student sleep landscape. Identified "Academic Crunch" — exam stress, assignment deadlines, and irregular schedules — as the critical missing variable in all existing health tracking tools. Existing apps had no concept of a student's calendar.

Phase 2 · Modeling
Engineering the Aura Algorithm

Designed the core scoring formula: a weighted 0–100 system incorporating sleep duration (optimal 7–9h window), caffeine intake penalties, screen time deductions, and mood/energy bonuses. The goal was to quantify qualitative data into a single actionable "readiness" number students could understand at a glance.

Phase 3 · Development
Building the Dashboard

Built using Python and Streamlit with a "Low-Friction" design philosophy — the entire logging experience is under 30 seconds. Integrated Plotly for interactive visualizations (trend charts, heatmaps, correlation scatterplots, bedtime timelines) and a SQLite backend for persistent local storage. Added an optional Anthropic API-powered AI Coach tab.

Phase 4 · Legacy
Open-Sourcing for the Future

Structured the codebase for community contribution and hosted it as an open-source repository, ensuring Sleep Aura remains a living tool. Any student or developer at York University and beyond can clone, customize, and improve the dashboard — removing financial barriers to wellness technology.

The heart of Sleep Aura is a weighted 0–100 scoring system that translates multiple behavioral inputs into a single "readiness" score — making complex data immediately understandable.

Optimal 7–9h window. Bell-curve peak at 8h with penalties for under/oversleeping.

Reflection & Critical Thinking

What I Learned

What I learned about the topic

Sleep science is deeply nuanced. I initially assumed duration was the only metric that mattered — but building the algorithm taught me that the interplay of caffeine timing, screen exposure, and emotional state can matter as much as hours logged. Data without context is just noise.

What I learned about myself as a learner

I work best when the problem feels personal. Knowing that the tool I was building could help friends and classmates — not just a hypothetical user — kept me motivated through the harder debugging sessions. I'm a problem-solver who needs a "why."

Key insights & "aha" moments

The biggest shift was realizing that the hardest part of wellness tech isn't the technology — it's the logging habit. This led to my "30-second design philosophy": every interaction must be fast enough that a tired student will actually do it before bed. That constraint made every design decision sharper.

Challenges & how I handled them

Displaying overnight sleep windows on a linear timeline was surprisingly tricky — a 11 PM bedtime and 7 AM wake need to "wrap" across midnight. I solved this by normalizing all times to a shifted 6 PM–noon (next day) window, treating pre-midnight hours as hours 18–24 and post-midnight as hours 24–36.

How my thinking changed

I started this project thinking I was building a tracker. I ended it realizing I had built a behavior-change tool. The difference is that a tracker shows you data; a behavior-change tool shows you data in a way that makes you want to act differently. That framing shift changed everything about how I designed the insights.

What I'd do differently next time

I'd conduct user testing with real students earlier in the process — even just five people doing one logging session would have revealed friction points I only discovered late. I'd also add push notifications or a browser reminder system to support the logging habit from day one.
